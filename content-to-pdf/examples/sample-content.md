# Executive Summary

The team increased release frequency in Q2, but the biggest improvement did not come from speed alone. It came from reducing ambiguity in handoff and review. Once planning, implementation, and reporting were expressed more clearly, the same team started shipping with fewer retries.

Three things mattered most:

- clearer ownership,
- smaller review loops,
- explicit follow-up after release.

# What Changed

## Planning

Planning moved from broad milestone language to narrower delivery slices. Teams stopped starting with "the whole feature" and instead scoped the first reviewable version earlier.

## Execution

Execution became easier once repeatable steps were written down. The team no longer relied on memory for release notes, rollout checks, or internal status updates.

## Review

Review quality improved when feedback focused on behavior, risk, and missing validation instead of broad stylistic comments.

# Key Metrics

| Metric | Q1 | Q2 | Change |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Weekly releases | 3 | 5 | +67% |
| Average review cycle | 2.8 days | 1.9 days | -32% |
| Rework after release | 11 items | 6 items | -45% |

> The most useful process improvements were not the most complex ones. They were the ones the team could repeat without needing extra explanation.

# Next Quarter Priorities

## Standardize Templates

The team should continue turning repeated workflows into templates so execution quality depends less on who happens to be online.

## Improve Reporting Rhythm

A shorter, more regular reporting rhythm will make it easier to spot drift before it becomes visible in delivery metrics.

## Build A Small Evaluation Set

It should become normal to review a small set of representative tasks after each workflow change so the team can catch regressions earlier.
